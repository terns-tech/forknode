Team,

Before we start discussing venues, branding, prizes, websites, or social media, I would like us to take a step back and ensure that we are building the project on a strong foundation.

A common mistake in event projects is to jump into logistics before there is complete clarity about the purpose and value proposition. The venue is important, but it is not what will make this initiative successful. Success will come from having a compelling proposition that attracts German companies, talented students, sponsors, and partners.

Our first priority should therefore be to clearly define what problem we are solving and for whom.

I would like us to answer five fundamental questions:

What problem are we solving?
For whom are we solving it?
Why is this problem important right now?
What measurable outcome will define success for us?
Why are we uniquely positioned to organize this initiative?


Once we have strong answers to these questions, creating different pitch decks for the different audiences, attracting partners, and planning the event itself becomes much easier.

Rather than creating multiple disconnected presentations, I recommend that we first develop one master narrative.

From that master narrative, we can then create stakeholder-specific pitch decks.

The first and most important deck should be for German companies. Why? Because the biggest risk in this project is not finding a venue. The biggest risk is investing significant effort and then discovering that German companies are not interested in participating, sponsoring, mentoring, judging, or recruiting.

We should validate demand from German companies as early as possible. (edited)
